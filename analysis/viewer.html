What this tool is trying to answer

One simple question: is there a better day and hour to post on Civitai? If you’re about to upload an image, does it matter whether you do it on a Tuesday afternoon or a Saturday night — or is it all the same?

To answer that honestly, the tool looks at thousands of the most-reacted images on Civitai, checks when each one was posted, and compares that against when images get posted in general. The gap between those two is the answer.

Where the data comes from

A companion script (analyze-posting-times.js) talks to Civitai’s public API and gathers two separate piles of information. It does not download any pictures — for each image it only notes a couple of plain facts.

Pile 1 — “the winners” (the Top pull)

It asks Civitai for its most-reacted images, working from the all-time champions downward, across every content rating (safe → explicit). For each one it records just two things:

  • When it was posted — the exact date and hour.
  • How many reactions it got — likes + hearts + laughs + cries added together.

Everything else — the image itself, the prompt, the author — is thrown away. These winners are then sorted into calendar-year buckets (2022, 2023, …), keeping roughly the top 5,000 of each year.

Pile 2 — “everybody” (the Baseline pull)

Then it grabs a big sample of recent uploads sorted by newest, completely ignoring how popular they are — the famous and the ignored, all mixed together. Again, only the timestamp is kept. This pile answers “when do people normally post?”

The key idea: “lift” (why we need the baseline)

Here’s the trap this tool exists to avoid. Imagine that 40% of all uploads happen on Saturday evening. Then naturally about 40% of the winners were also posted Saturday evening — but that’s just because that’s when everyone posts, not because it’s a magic time.

So instead of “when were the winners posted?”, the tool computes lift:

lift = (winners’ share of a day/hour) ÷ (everybody’s share of that day/hour)

  • lift > 1 (shown red) → that slot over-performs: winners land there more than raw posting volume would predict. A genuinely good time to post.
  • lift < 1 (shown blue) → that slot under-performs.
  • lift ≈ 1 → no advantage either way.

That single ratio is the honest core of the whole tool.

How to read each chart

⏰ When to post

  • Day × hour heatmap — a whole week as a grid (7 rows = days, 24 columns = hours). In Lift mode, red squares are good times, blue are weak. The Top volume and Baseline buttons show the raw counts behind the lift, if you’re curious.
  • Best hour of day — the same lift, collapsed into 24 bars so you can see which hours win. Bars above the dashed 1× line over-perform.
  • Best day of week — lift collapsed into 7 weekday bars.
  • The cards at the top spell out the single best slot, hour and day for the year you’ve selected.

📊 The data (years & months)

  • Timeline — one bar per month across every year. This is the platform’s rise and fall: you can literally see the boom and the decline. Toggle between image count and median reactions.
  • Top images per month — the selected year broken into its 12 months.
  • Reaction-size distribution — how big the winners were that year (how many landed in 1k–2.5k reactions, 5k–10k, etc.).
  • Year-over-year table — median/mean/peak reactions per year, plus whether that year’s sample is “full” or “partial”.

A few things to keep in mind (honesty notes)

  • All times are UTC. Use the Timezone selector at the top to shift the time-based charts to your own local hours.
  • The month / timeline charts are raw counts, not lift. The baseline only covers recent weeks, so it can’t fairly judge “best month” of years past. Treat those as “here’s what was collected”, and treat the day/hour lift as the real “best time” answer.
  • Older images had longer to collect reactions. That mainly affects comparing reaction sizes across years (the boom/decline magnitude), not the within-year day/hour pattern.
  • We only look at winners. Lift controls for posting volume, but it can’t tell you why a slot wins (more people online? the feed algorithm? luck on a small sample).
  • The baseline’s weekly rhythm is assumed steady over time. Reasonable, but an assumption — not a measurement.
  • Before you load real data, the numbers are fake. The viewer ships with a synthetic demo (with a planted “Tue–Thu afternoon wins” pattern) just to show the layout.

Refreshing the data

To get fresh numbers, re-run the pull where it has internet access to Civitai (your PC or a GitHub Action):

node analyze-posting-times.js

That writes posting-analysis.json (what this page loads) and posting-raw.json (the slim raw data). After that first pull, any future view change can be recomputed instantly — with no second trip to the API — using node analyze-posting-times.js --in posting-raw.json. Then load the JSON here via Choose File.
